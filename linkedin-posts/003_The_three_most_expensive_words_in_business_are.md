The three most expensive words in business are:
 


"For now, I'll wait."
 
It sounds responsible. It usually isn't.
 
I've heard the same three versions of it hundreds of times from founders.
 
Almost always from founders who know their business should be growing faster.
 
The first:
 
"Let me try it on my own first."
 
Usually means:
 
"I'll spend the next few months using the same approach, with the same blind spots, hoping the outcome changes."
 
The second:
 
"I've already invested elsewhere."
 
Usually means:
 
"I'm still attached to the money I spent, even though the business still has the same problem."
 
The third:
 
"Referrals have always worked."
 
Usually means:
 
"People want what I sell. I just don't control when the next opportunity appears."
 
Different founders with different offers and at different stages.

The business works.
The founder can sell.
 
But growth depends too much on:
 
→ timing → referrals → memory 
→ following up when they remember
 
That gets expensive surprisingly quickly.
 
Leads go cold.
Conversations drift.
Good offers become easy to forget.
 
And when you're a solo founder, there isn't a sales team creating opportunities behind the scenes.

It's just you.
 
Which is why client acquisition has to become a system.
 
Not a hope.
 
Growth doesn't mean being better at what you do.
 
It means building a system for the right buyers to remember you when the problem becomes urgent.
 
Because buyers rarely choose the first person they see.
 
They choose the person they remember.
