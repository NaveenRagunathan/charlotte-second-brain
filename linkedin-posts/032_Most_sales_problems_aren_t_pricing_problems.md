Most sales problems aren’t pricing problems.
 
They’re positioning problems.
 
Steve Jobs understood this before most founders understand sales.
 
Apple didn’t win because it had better specs.
 
It won because it made decisions easier.
 
74% of buyers choose the option that feels simplest.

Apple didn’t win because it had more features.
It won because it sold clarity.

6 Steve Jobs principles that apply directly to premium sales:

𝟭. 𝗥𝘂𝘁𝗵𝗹𝗲𝘀𝘀 𝗙𝗼𝗰𝘂𝘀 
↳ “Deciding what not to do as important as deciding what to do.”
❌ Selling everything to everyone 
✅ One clear offer solving one specific problem

Clarity converts. Complexity stalls.

𝟮. 𝗦𝗶𝗺𝗽𝗹𝗶𝗰𝗶𝘁𝘆 𝗶𝘀 𝗣𝗼𝘄𝗲𝗿 
↳ “Simple can be harder than complex.”
❌ Overloaded decks. Endless features. 
✅ 1 clear problem. 1 clear outcome. 1 clear next step.

If it feels complicated, it won’t close.

𝟯. 𝗦𝗲𝗹𝗹 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗩𝗶𝘀𝗶𝗼𝗻, 𝗡𝗼𝘁 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗦𝗽𝗲𝗰𝘀 
↳ People didn’t buy an MP3 player.
❌ “32GB storage. Advanced interface.” 
✅ “1,000 songs in your pocket.”

Clients don’t buy hours. They buy certainty. Control. 

𝟰. 𝗢𝗯𝘀𝗲𝘀𝘀 𝗢𝘃𝗲𝗿 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗖𝘂𝘀𝘁𝗼𝗺𝗲𝗿 𝗘𝘅𝗽𝗲𝗿𝗶𝗲𝗻𝗰𝗲 
↳ Jobs controlled the entire ecosystem.
❌ Sloppy follow-ups. Delayed replies. Clunkiness. 
✅ Clean communication. Fast decisions. Seamless delivery.

Authority is felt in the details.

𝟱. 𝗙𝗲𝘄𝗲𝗿 𝗣𝗿𝗼𝗱𝘂𝗰𝘁𝘀. 𝗛𝗶𝗴𝗵𝗲𝗿 𝗦𝘁𝗮𝗻𝗱𝗮𝗿𝗱𝘀. 
↳ Apple launches less. But when it does, it dominates.
❌ Multiple low-conviction offers 
✅ One sharp offer with full commitment

Premium isn’t volume. It’s precision.

𝟲. 𝗢𝘄𝗻 𝘁𝗵𝗲 𝗡𝗮𝗿𝗿𝗮𝘁𝗶𝘃𝗲 
↳ Jobs didn’t wait for the market to define Apple. He defined it.
❌ Letting buyers position you as a vendor 
✅ Positioning yourself as the only logical choice

If you don’t control the story, price becomes the story.
Your choice.

Steve Jobs didn’t “sell harder.”
He removed noise. Raised standards. 
And made decisions easier.

That’s what great selling actually is.
