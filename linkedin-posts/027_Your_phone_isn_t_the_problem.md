Your phone isn't the problem
Your dependency on it is

Your phone isn't stealing your time 
Its training you to be a worse...

Because it conditions you to avoid depth.

Founders say they want better sales…
 
Then they show up distracted, impatient, half-listening: 

Already thinking about the next thing before the buyer has finished the sentence.
 
That’s not a small habit.
 
It changes how you run the entire business.
 
Because great selling isn’t about talking well.
 
It’s about noticing well.
 
The pause after you say the price. 
The slight change in tone. 
The hesitation hidden inside: 

“Sounds good… send it over.”
 
The moment a buyer stops feeling heard and starts feeling handled.
 
A fried attention span misses all of that.
 
So does a founder living on constant stimulation.
 
You don’t just lose focus.
 
You lose sharpness. 
You lose patience. 

You lose the ability to sit in discomfort long enough to find the real problem.
 
And then the business pays for it.
 
Sales calls feel rushed. Meetings get noisy. Decisions become reactive.
 
The team gets less clarity. The buyer gets less trust.
 
And most of this happens quietly.
 
That’s why it’s dangerous.
 
Nobody says: “Your attention is becoming a leadership liability.”
 
They just stop buying. 
Or stop trusting your judgment. 
Or stop bringing you the hard stuff 

Because you don’t seem fully there.
 
I’ve felt this in myself too.
 
More scattered. More urgency. 
More impulse. Less depth.
 
So now the phone goes away when I’m working and before bed.
 
Because when your brain gets hooked on speed
Your business starts to reflect it.
 
More motion. Less traction. 
More activity. Less progress. 
More talking. Less listening.
 
A lot of founders think they have a marketing problem.
 
Some of them have an attention problem.
 
And that attention problem is showing up in how they sell.
 
Where do you feel this first in business:
Sales, decision-making, or leadership?
