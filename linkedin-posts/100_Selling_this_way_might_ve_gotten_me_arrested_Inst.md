Selling this way might’ve gotten me arrested. Instead, it got the deal.

I wouldn’t recommend it. 
But it taught me everything I know about follow-up.

Back in my corporate sales days, 10 years ago,
I was trying to close a deal with a government official.

They’d gone quiet. 
Wouldn’t respond to emails. 
I’d followed up — nothing.

But I knew, from our first conversation, they were keen. 
They just needed a nudge.

Government approvals came with layers of complexity.

Myself and the prospect were both attending an event at the World Bank. 

I bumped into him coming out of a building.

Hurried, there was no time for a debrief on the deal.

So I took a guess where he was staying and said:

“You’re probably staying at the Four Seasons 
Most officials are.”

Then I showed up. 
Gutsy.

I know… it sounds extreme. 

I felt like a stalker. 
But I also knew they wanted to move forward.

I saw them heading to their room. 
Got in the elevator. Introduced myself. 
And asked directly: 

“Where are we at with the deal?”

I braced for an awkward moment — or a shutdown.
Instead, they smiled: 
“I’m glad you came to find me. I like the persistence.”

The deal? Eventually closed. Yes in principle on the spot.

Now let me be clear: I don’t recommend hotel drop-ins.
But that moment taught me something invaluable.

And what I now teach my clients:

Most deals aren’t lost because your offer isn’t good. 
They’re lost because you didn’t follow up enough. 
Or because you waited for them to take the next step.

Persistence isn’t pushy - it’s proof you care.
And for coaches and consultants? 
You don’t need to be extreme.

But you do need to:

Take the lead confidently. 
Aim to close on the first call 
Follow up more than once if needed. 

Stay in the conversation, especially when it goes quiet.

Your people want to buy. 

But they need you to lead the decision, not wait for it.
That’s not pressure. That’s service. 

My clients are landing $20K+ deals with strategic follow-up.
