At 18, I almost died. The experience changed how I handle business fear.
I was held at knifepoint. 

It taught me the difference between: 
Real danger and imaginary fear.

Sat in a car at traffic light. Heart pounding. 
Mind racing. It was terrifying.

The lights flicked to green, we escaped into the night.
I literally froze for the rest of the journey.
I was living in a foreign country at the time.

That moment taught me valuable lessons in my 
24-year sales career and now business.

More so when facing what we think is "dangerous."

Real danger: A knife to your throat. 
Imaginary fear: Asking for a higher price.

Real danger: Your life is threatened. 
Imaginary fear: Someone saying no to your offer.

Real danger: Physical harm. 
Imaginary fear: Looking stupid on a sales call.

After surviving actual danger, I realized: 
Most of what we call "fear" in business is just discomfort.

We've convinced ourselves that rejection is dangerous. 
That charging premium rates is risky. 
That cold outreach might "hurt" us.

But none of these things can really harm you. 
They can't threaten your life. 
They can only bruise your ego.

Once you know the difference between real danger and imaginary fear, anything is possible.

You have difficult conversations. 
You ask for what you want.

Business fears can't hurt you.
They can only help you grow.

If you're letting imaginary fears keep you small, it's time to face them. The knife was real. Your sales call isn't.

This is exactly the mindset shift I help my clients master.
Turning fear into fuel for bigger conversations and better results.
